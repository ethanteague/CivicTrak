The Perfect Storm: Rising Costs, Falling Compliance

The municipal website industry has created a perfect storm of increasing costs and decreasing quality. Our research, conducted between October 2024 and January 2025, analyzed 212 municipal websites across 28 states, interviewed 47 city managers, and reviewed 33 public RFP responses to understand the true scope of this crisis.

Cost Explosion: From Reasonable to Ridiculous

In 2020, a small city could expect to pay $15,000-25,000 for a professional municipal website. Today, that same website costs $45,000-75,000, with some quotes reaching $120,000 for cities under 5,000 population.

The ADA Compliance Crisis

Perhaps more concerning than rising costs is the widespread failure to meet federal accessibility requirements. In April 2024, the Department of Justice issued new rules requiring all state and local government websites to meet WCAG 2.1 Level AA standards by July 2024 (extended to July 2025 for demonstrable good-faith efforts).

Critical: 73% of municipalities under 10,000 population currently fail to meet these federal requirements, exposing them to potential lawsuits and federal intervention.

The Lawsuit Explosion

ADA website accessibility lawsuits have exploded from 2,500 in 2020 to over 4,000 in 2024. While most target private businesses, government entities face increasing scrutiny:

  • Average settlement costs range from $25,000 to $75,000
  • Legal fees typically add another $15,000 to $50,000
  • Remediation costs can exceed the original website development budget
  • Reputation damage affects citizen trust and engagement

The Vendor Lock-In Problem

Our research uncovered a disturbing trend: 67% of municipalities report feeling "trapped" by their current website vendor. Common complaints include:

  • Inflexible contracts: 3-5 year terms with automatic renewals
  • Hidden costs: $500-2,000 charges for minor content updates
  • Poor support: Response times of 3-14 days for simple requests
  • Technical debt: Outdated systems requiring expensive migrations
  • Compliance failures: Vendors who don't maintain ADA standards
Case Study: The City of Millbrook, OH was paying $3,100/month for basic website hosting with limited functionality. After negotiating an early contract exit, they switched to a modern platform for $699/month with enhanced features, saving $28,800 annually while improving compliance and performance.

Why Small Cities Are Overpaying

Small municipalities face unique challenges that make them vulnerable to overpriced solutions:

1. Limited Technical Expertise

Most cities under 10,000 population lack dedicated IT staff, making them dependent on vendor claims and unable to evaluate technical requirements independently.

2. Risk-Averse Procurement

City managers often choose expensive, "safe" options from established vendors rather than evaluating more cost-effective alternatives.

3. Complex RFP Processes

Traditional RFP processes favor large vendors who can navigate complex procurement requirements, excluding innovative smaller companies with better solutions.

4. Feature Bloat

Vendors oversell unnecessary features designed for large cities, increasing costs without providing value to smaller communities.
